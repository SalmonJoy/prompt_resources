1Workshop overview

This is a 3.5-hour advanced workshop. It assumes you already use ChatGPT and does not repeat a basic introduction to generative AI. Instead, it focuses on the skills that separate an average prompt from a reliable, workplace-ready one: framing the task, grounding the model in the right information, and engineering the result through critique, refinement and validation.

How the time is used

3 hoursAdvanced prompt engineering with ChatGPT
30 minutesCreating presentations with Gamma AI

Who this is for

Non-technical and semi-technical professionals across Manufacturing, QA/QC, R&D, HR, L&D, IT, Administration, Supply Chain, Finance and other corporate departments. No coding knowledge is required at any point.

How to use this handbook

  • During the workshop: follow along with the techniques as they are demonstrated.
  • During exercises: use the weak / improved / follow-up patterns as models for your own prompts.
  • After the workshop: keep it as a desk reference; return to the framework and templates often.
  • In practice: prefer fictional or sanitised examples until you know what is approved for AI use.
Remember
A better prompt is not a longer prompt. It is a clearer instruction, grounded in the right information, with the output and the checks defined in advance.

2Learning objectives

By the end of this workshop you should be able to:

  • Frame a workplace request as a clear, solvable task before prompting.
  • Supply the right context and approved source information, and keep it separate from your instructions.
  • Use positive and negative constraints to control what ChatGPT includes, excludes and assumes.
  • Specify the structure, length and tone of the output in advance.
  • Run a multi-step workflow: generate, critique, improve, validate and finalise.
  • Adapt the same content for different audiences without rewriting from scratch.
  • Recognise hallucination risks and verify facts before use.
  • Apply responsible-use rules consistently, using fictional or sanitised examples for practice.
  • Turn a reviewed ChatGPT outline into a Gamma presentation efficiently.
Set your own goal
Pick one repetitive task you do every week. Aim to leave the workshop with a reusable, reviewed prompt for exactly that task.

3The three advanced themes

Everything in this handbook is organised around three themes. Together they form the workshop model: Frame the Problem → Ground the Model → Engineer the Result.

Theme 1 · Architect the Task

Identify the real business objective, define the audience, describe the expected result, break complex requirements into stages, identify missing information, control assumptions, and ask ChatGPT to clarify the requirement before it answers.

Key message: “Frame the problem before asking AI to solve it.”

Theme 2 · Engineer the Context

Provide relevant background, supply approved source information, separate instructions from source content, use positive and negative constraints, provide examples when useful, define instruction priorities, and reduce hallucination and unsupported assumptions.

Key message: “Control the information environment, not only the wording of the prompt.”

Theme 3 · Orchestrate the Output

Generate an initial draft, evaluate it against specific criteria, critique its weaknesses, refine it through follow-up prompts, adapt content for different audiences, check factual support, and validate the final output before use.

Key message: “The first response is generated. The desired response is engineered.”

How the themes work together
Theme 1 decides what you are asking for. Theme 2 decides what information the model may use. Theme 3 decides how you turn a draft into something you trust. Skipping any one of them is the usual reason workplace outputs feel generic, inaccurate or unfinished.

4The advanced prompt framework

Use these ten elements as your mental checklist. You will not need every element every time, but the more of them you include deliberately, the more reliable the output.

  1. 1. Goal What business outcome is required?
  2. 2. Role What perspective should ChatGPT adopt?
  3. 3. Context What background does ChatGPT need?
  4. 4. Source input What information should ChatGPT use?
  5. 5. Audience Who will read or use the output?
  6. 6. Instructions What specific actions should ChatGPT perform?
  7. 7. Constraints What must be included, excluded, limited or not assumed?
  8. 8. Output format What structure, length, tone and detail are required?
  9. 9. Quality criteria What characteristics define a good output?
  10. 10. Validation What must be checked before using the output?

The reusable prompt structure

This structure appears throughout the pack. Copy it, then replace the bracketed placeholders.

Reusable structureAct as [ROLE]. Goal: [DESIRED BUSINESS OUTCOME] Context: [RELEVANT BACKGROUND] Source information: Use only the information enclosed within the source tags. <source> [SOURCE INFORMATION] </source> Audience: [INTENDED AUDIENCE] Instructions: - [ACTION 1] - [ACTION 2] - [ACTION 3] Constraints: - Do not add unsupported information. - Clearly identify missing information. - Do not make unverified assumptions. - [ADDITIONAL CONSTRAINT] Output format: [REQUIRED STRUCTURE] Tone: [REQUIRED TONE] Quality criteria: - [CRITERION 1] - [CRITERION 2] - [CRITERION 3] Before finalising: Check the response for unsupported claims, missing information, instruction compliance, clarity and audience suitability.
Remember
The framework is a checklist, not a script. Keep the labels (Goal, Context, Source, and so on) so both you and ChatGPT can see the structure clearly.

5Task framing and requirement clarification

What it means

Framing means turning a vague request into a precise task: naming the real objective, the audience and what a successful output looks like, before you write a single instruction.

When to use it

Always — but especially when the request came to you as one line (“send something to the team about the delay”) or when several people will read the output.

Weak promptWrite something about the line stoppage.
Improved promptAct as a production coordinator. Goal: Inform the packing team about a planned two-hour stoppage on a packing line so they can adjust their schedule. Audience: Shift operators and the shift in-charge. Write a short internal note (120-150 words) covering: what is happening, when, the expected impact, and what the team should do in the meantime. Constraints: Do not invent a cause or a restart time. If either is unknown, mark it clearly as "to be confirmed".
Follow-up promptGood. Now add a one-line summary at the top that a supervisor could read in five seconds, and flag any information I still need to confirm before sending.

Workplace example

A fictional packing-line coordinator receives “tell the team about the stoppage.” After framing, the real task is: inform operators and the shift in-charge of a planned two-hour pause, what they should do meanwhile, and what remains unconfirmed. The framed prompt produces a short note that a busy floor can act on without inventing a cause or restart time.

Exercise
Take a request you received this week as a single sentence. Write down its real objective, its audience, and one sentence describing a successful output. Only then draft the prompt.
Remember
If you cannot describe a good output in one sentence, ChatGPT cannot either. Frame first.

6Breaking complex tasks into stages

What it means

Splitting a large request into smaller, ordered steps so each step is easy to check before moving on.

When to use it

When a task has several parts (analyse, then draft, then format), or when a single prompt keeps producing output that is partly right and partly wrong.

Weak promptAnalyse these meeting notes and give me the full minutes, an action tracker, and a management summary.
Improved promptWe will work in three steps and I will confirm each before the next. Step 1: From the notes below, list every decision and every action item with an owner. Ask me about anything ambiguous instead of guessing. <source>[PASTE NOTES]</source>
Follow-up promptConfirmed. Step 2: Turn the confirmed list into formal minutes with sections Attendees, Decisions, Actions (owner and due date), and Open questions.

Workplace example

A fictional cross-functional meeting produced messy notes covering decisions, actions and open questions. Asking for minutes, an action tracker and a management summary in one prompt mixed errors across all three. Staging the work — extract first, confirm, then format — kept each deliverable accurate.

Exercise
Write down a three-step plan for a report you produce regularly. Decide what you will check at the end of each step before continuing.
Remember
Small steps are easier to verify. Confirm each stage before you build on it.

7Asking ChatGPT clarifying questions

What it means

Instructing ChatGPT to ask you questions before it answers, so it fills gaps with your information rather than its assumptions.

When to use it

At the start of any non-trivial task, or whenever you are not sure you have given enough detail.

Weak promptDraft a policy communication about the new leave process.
Improved promptI need to draft a policy communication about a new leave-application process. Before you write anything, ask me up to five questions that would materially change the draft (audience, effective date, what is changing, tone, and length). Wait for my answers.
Follow-up promptHere are my answers: [ANSWERS]. Now draft the communication using only these details, and mark anything you still had to assume.

Workplace example

An HR colleague preparing a fictional leave-process update asked ChatGPT to interview them first. The model asked about effective date, who must apply the new process, exceptions, and tone. Those answers prevented a polished draft that would have invented an effective date.

Exercise
Ask ChatGPT to interview you before drafting a routine email. Note which of its questions you had not thought about.
Remember
A few good questions up front prevent a confident but wrong answer later.

8Context engineering

What it means

Deliberately shaping the information environment: what background you give, what you exclude, and how you organise it — not just how you word the request.

When to use it

Whenever the quality of the answer depends on facts ChatGPT would not otherwise know, such as your situation, your constraints or your existing material.

Weak promptWrite a status update for my project.
Improved promptContext: This is a weekly status update for an internal system-upgrade project. Last week we completed user testing; this week we are preparing training; a vendor dependency is delayed by a few days. The reader is a steering group that wants risks and decisions, not detail. Write a one-page update with sections: Progress, Risks, Decisions needed, Next week.
Follow-up promptTighten the Risks section so each risk has an impact and a proposed mitigation, and remove anything that is just routine progress.

Workplace example

An IT project lead drafting a fictional weekly steering update added two lines of context: testing is done, training is next, and a vendor delay of a few days is the main risk. With that background, the draft focused on decisions rather than inventing a full project history.

Exercise
Rewrite one of your recent prompts by adding a two-line context paragraph. Compare the two outputs.
Remember
Control the information environment, not only the wording of the prompt.

9Source-grounded prompting

What it means

Giving ChatGPT the exact material it should rely on and instructing it to use only that material, clearly separated from your instructions using tags.

When to use it

For summaries, rewrites and extractions where accuracy to a specific document matters. This is one of the strongest ways to reduce invented detail.

Weak promptSummarise our returns policy.
Improved promptSummarise the returns policy below in five bullet points for a new team member. Use only the information inside the source tags. If the policy does not cover something, say "not specified in the source" rather than filling the gap. <source> [PASTE THE APPROVED POLICY TEXT] </source>
Follow-up promptList any questions a new team member might ask that the source does not answer, so I can raise them with the policy owner.

Workplace example

An L&D facilitator summarised a fictional, approved visitor-badge procedure for new joiners. With source tags and a “use only this source” constraint, the summary stayed faithful. Without them, the draft invented a visitor escort rule that was never in the document.

Exercise
Take a short, non-confidential document and summarise it twice: once without source tags and once with them. Note the difference in invented detail.
Responsible use
Only paste information that is approved for use in a public AI tool. Use fictional or sanitised text when practising.
Remember
“Use only the information in the source” is one of the most useful sentences in prompting.

10Positive and negative constraints

What it means

Positive constraints say what must be included; negative constraints say what must be excluded, limited or not assumed.

When to use it

When outputs drift off-topic, become too long, adopt the wrong tone, or add claims you cannot support.

Weak promptWrite an announcement about the office move.
Improved promptWrite a staff announcement about an office move. Must include: the move date, the new location name, and who to contact with questions. Must not include: specific desk allocations, any commitments about parking, or any date I have not given you. Keep it under 150 words and neutral in tone.
Follow-up promptAdd a short reassuring closing line, but do not promise anything beyond the facts above.

Workplace example

Facilities drafted a fictional office-move announcement. Positive constraints forced inclusion of the move date, location name and contact. Negative constraints blocked desk allocations and parking promises that had not been decided. The result was short, useful and safe to send.

Exercise
List three “must include” and three “must not include” items for a message you send often. Turn them into constraints.
Remember
Telling ChatGPT what not to do is as powerful as telling it what to do.

11Audience-aware prompting

What it means

Naming exactly who will read the output so ChatGPT can match vocabulary, detail and tone to that reader.

When to use it

Any time the same information must reach different readers — operators, managers, or an external audience.

Weak promptExplain the new scheduling tool.
Improved promptExplain the new scheduling tool in two versions. Version A for shop-floor operators: plain language, focus on what changes in their daily routine, under 120 words. Version B for department heads: focus on benefits and the rollout timeline, under 120 words.
Follow-up promptNow produce a third version for an all-staff email that combines the key points without repeating them.

Workplace example

A supply-chain team explained a fictional new scheduling tool. The operator version focused on daily routine changes; the department-head version focused on benefits and rollout timing. Same facts, different emphasis — and neither version invented features.

Exercise
Take one message and ask for two audience versions. Compare how the wording changes.
Remember
“Who is reading this?” changes the answer more than almost any other instruction.

12Example-based prompting

What it means

Showing ChatGPT one or two short examples of the style or structure you want, so it matches your pattern instead of guessing.

When to use it

When you have a house style, a preferred format, or a “good” example you want future outputs to resemble.

Weak promptWrite a handover note.
Improved promptWrite a handover note in the same style as this example. Match the headings and the level of detail. Example: "Status: On track. Open items: 2 (see below). Watch-outs: vendor call pending. Next: confirm delivery slot." Now write one for today using these facts: [FACTS].
Follow-up promptShorten each line to a single sentence and keep the same four headings.

Workplace example

A manufacturing supervisor pasted a short fictional handover example with four headings — Status, Open items, Watch-outs, Next — and asked ChatGPT to match that pattern for today’s facts. The output needed almost no reformatting before use.

Exercise
Find one output you are happy with. Paste a shortened version as an example and ask ChatGPT to match it.
Remember
One good example is often clearer than three sentences of description.

13Structured output prompting

What it means

Telling ChatGPT the exact shape of the output — a table with named columns, a fixed set of headings, a word limit, or a numbered list.

When to use it

Whenever the output will be reused, compared or pasted into another document or template.

Weak promptGive me the pros and cons of the two options.
Improved promptCompare Option A and Option B in a table with these columns: Criterion, Option A, Option B, Notes. Use these rows: Cost, Time to implement, Effort required, Main risk. Keep each cell under 15 words. After the table, add a one-line neutral summary.
Follow-up promptAdd a "Recommendation to discuss" row, but frame it as an option for human decision, not a final decision.

Workplace example

Finance asked for a comparison of two fictional vendor options. Specifying a table with columns Criterion, Option A, Option B and Notes produced a reusable comparison. A free-form “pros and cons” list had been harder to paste into a briefing pack.

Exercise
Rewrite a recent request so it specifies a table or a fixed set of headings. Note how much easier the output is to reuse.
Remember
If you know the shape of the answer, describe it. ChatGPT is good at filling a structure you define.

14Multi-step prompt workflows

What it means

Using a sequence of connected prompts, where each response becomes the input for the next, instead of trying to get everything from one prompt.

When to use it

For anything you want to polish: drafts that need structure, then tone, then a final tidy-up.

Weak promptTurn these bullet points into a perfect department update that is structured, managerial and ready to send.
Workflow · four connected prompts1) Draft: "Draft a department update from these bullet points: [POINTS]." 2) Structure: "Reorganise into Progress, Risks, Next steps. Remove repetition." 3) Tone: "Make it concise and professional for a manager. Keep it under 200 words." 4) Final: "Give me the final clean version with a one-line summary at the top."
Follow-up promptList anything in this final version that I should verify myself before sending.

Workplace example

An administration lead turned rough fictional bullets about a facilities upgrade into a manager update by running draft → structure → tone → final. Each step was easier to check than one overloaded prompt that tried to do everything at once.

Exercise
Take one document through the four steps above. Notice which step improved it most.
Remember
Polish comes through conversation, not a single perfect prompt.

15The Generate–Critique–Improve–Validate–Finalise workflow

What it means

This five-stage cycle is the core of Theme 3. You generate a draft, critique it against criteria, improve it, validate the facts yourself, then finalise. Apply it to any output that matters.

When to use it

Whenever the output will be shared, filed, or used to brief others — and whenever the first draft is “almost right” but not yet trustworthy.

  1. Generate — produce a first draft with a well-framed prompt.
  2. Critique — ask ChatGPT to critique its own draft against your criteria.
  3. Improve — ask it to rewrite, fixing the weaknesses it just identified.
  4. Validate — check facts, references and instruction compliance yourself.
  5. Finalise — request the clean final version and do a last human read.
Weak promptMake this draft better.
Critique promptCritique your previous draft against these criteria: instruction compliance, factual support, clarity, and suitability for the audience. List the three weakest points. Do not rewrite it yet.
Improve promptNow rewrite the draft to fix those three points. Keep everything that was already working.

Workplace example

QA used a fictional observation summary through this cycle. Critique revealed an unsupported severity claim and a missing open item. Improve removed the claim and restored the item. Validate confirmed every line against the notes before the summary was shared for discussion — not as an official conclusion.

Exercise
Take one draft through all five stages. Write down what changed at Critique and what you personally checked at Validate.
Remember
The first response is generated. The desired response is engineered.

16Using evaluation rubrics

What it means

Defining, in advance, the criteria a good output must meet, then scoring the draft against them.

When to use it

For repeated outputs where you want consistent quality, or when several people judge “good” differently.

Weak promptIs this good enough?
Rubric promptEvaluate the draft against this rubric and give each a rating of Strong, Adequate or Weak with one reason: - Follows all instructions - Uses only supported information - Clear for the stated audience - Correct structure and length - Appropriate tone Then list the single most important fix.
Follow-up promptApply only the most important fix and show the updated section.

Workplace example

An L&D designer scored a fictional session outline against a five-line rubric before sharing it with a facilitator. The rubric flagged weak learning objectives; one targeted fix raised the quality without a full rewrite.

Exercise
Write a five-line rubric for a document you produce weekly. Use it to score your next AI draft.
Remember
A rubric turns “make it better” into specific, checkable improvements.

17Creating multiple versions

What it means

Asking for several distinct options at once, then combining or choosing, instead of settling for the first attempt.

When to use it

For subject lines, openings, summaries, or any wording where you want choice.

Weak promptWrite a subject line for this email.
Improved promptGive me five subject lines for this email: two neutral, two more direct, one very short. Keep each under 8 words. Then recommend which suits a busy manager and why.
Follow-up promptCombine the best parts of options 2 and 5 into one final subject line.

Workplace example

IT needed a subject line for a fictional password-reset service notice. Asking for five options (neutral, direct, short) gave a clear choice. Combining the best two produced a line managers actually opened.

Exercise
Ask for three openings for your next update and pick the strongest to build on.
Remember
Options are cheap to generate. Ask for a few and choose deliberately.

18Adapting content for different audiences

What it means

Reusing one reviewed piece of content and reshaping it for a new reader, length or channel.

When to use it

When the same message must go to operators, managers and an all-staff channel.

Weak promptRewrite this for everyone.
Improved promptHere is an approved paragraph: [PASTE]. Produce three adaptations without adding new facts: 1) A two-line operator instruction. 2) A short manager summary focused on impact. 3) A one-sentence notice-board line.
Follow-up promptCheck that none of the three versions introduced any detail that was not in the original paragraph.

Workplace example

R&D adapted a short, fictional, already-reviewed technical note into an operator instruction, a manager summary and a notice-board line. Because the source paragraph was fixed, none of the adaptations invented new technical claims.

Exercise
Take one approved paragraph and produce three audience adaptations. Confirm no new facts crept in.
Remember
Adapt reviewed content; do not regenerate facts each time.

19Identifying unsupported assumptions

What it means

Spotting where ChatGPT has filled a gap with a guess presented as fact, and forcing those gaps into the open.

When to use it

Before using any output that contains numbers, names, dates, references or specific claims.

Weak promptClean this up and fill in anything missing.
Improved promptReview your draft and mark every statement that is not directly supported by the source I gave you. For each, say whether it is an assumption, a generalisation, or a possible error. Do not fix them yet — just list them.
Follow-up promptRewrite the draft so that unsupported statements are either removed or clearly marked as "to be confirmed".

Workplace example

A meeting secretary noticed a fictional minutes draft had invented an owner for an open action. Asking the model to list unsupported statements surfaced the invented owner before the minutes were circulated.

Exercise
Ask ChatGPT to flag its own unsupported statements in a recent draft. Decide which to remove and which to verify.
Remember
Make assumptions visible. A marked gap is safe; a hidden one is not.

21Hallucination and factual-verification risks

What it means

A hallucination is when ChatGPT states something incorrect with full confidence — an invented figure, a plausible-sounding reference, or a detail that was never in your source.

Why it happens

The model predicts likely text; it does not know what is true, and it does not have real-time knowledge of recent changes. Confidence in the wording is not evidence of accuracy.

How to reduce and catch it

  • Ground the prompt in an approved source and instruct “use only this source”.
  • Ask it to mark anything not supported by the source.
  • Independently verify every figure, name, date and reference.
  • Treat references and citations as unverified until you have checked them.
  • Keep uncertainty visible rather than letting the draft smooth it over.
  • Prefer “to be confirmed” over a plausible invented detail.
  • Never ask ChatGPT to invent missing owners, dates, root causes or approvals.

Quick verification habit

Before you send or file an AI-assisted draft, underline every concrete claim (number, name, date, reference, cause). For each underline, ask: Where did this come from — my source, my knowledge, or the model? If you cannot answer, verify or remove it.

Do not rely on ChatGPT as an authority
ChatGPT must not be treated as an authority for regulatory, quality, clinical, financial or legal decisions. Those require qualified human judgement and approved processes.
Remember
Confident wording is not proof. Verify before you use.

22Responsible workplace use

Responsible use protects you, your colleagues and your organisation. Apply these principles every time. This section is workshop guidance, not an official company policy.

Core principles

  • AI output is a draft unless reviewed.
  • Human subject-matter experts remain responsible.
  • Important facts must be independently verified.
  • Internal company policy takes precedence.
  • Use fictional and anonymised examples when practising.

Keep out of public AI tools without approval

Confidential, personal, proprietary, audit-sensitive, clinical, formula-related, regulatory or credential information must not be entered into public AI tools without organisational approval. When in doubt, ask before you paste.

Safer practice habits

  • Practise with fictional scenarios from Resources 05 and 06.
  • Anonymise names, batch identifiers and site details before any practice upload.
  • Do not ask ChatGPT for CAPA decisions, GMP approvals, clinical conclusions or regulatory advice.
  • Keep final accountability with the human user and approved processes.
Policy precedence
This handbook provides general workshop guidance. Your organisation’s approved information-security, data-privacy, quality, regulatory and AI-usage policies take precedence. See Resource 04 for the full responsible-AI checklist.

23ChatGPT-to-Gamma workflow

Gamma AI turns an outline into a designed presentation quickly. The reliable pattern is to build and review the content in ChatGPT first, then generate the deck in Gamma. Design cannot repair inaccurate or unordered content — so do the thinking before you generate slides.

  1. Define the presentation objective and the single message the audience should remember.
  2. Identify the audience and the number of slides or cards.
  3. Ask ChatGPT for a slide-by-slide outline (title, one key message, up to three points, a suggested visual).
  4. Review the outline for facts, logical flow and duplication.
  5. Convert the outline into short Gamma-ready lines (under 12 words per bullet where possible).
  6. Paste the reviewed outline into Gamma to generate the presentation.
  7. Choose a suitable theme, edit layouts, replace unsuitable visuals and reduce excess text.
  8. Check consistency of terminology, tone and capitalisation across cards.
  9. Do a final human review before sharing or exporting.
Full detail in Resource 07
The ChatGPT-to-Gamma Quick Guide contains the complete 13-step workflow, a reusable prompt, a six-slide sample structure and pre- and post-Gamma checklists.
Remember
The outline leads; the template follows. Never let a theme decide your storyline.
