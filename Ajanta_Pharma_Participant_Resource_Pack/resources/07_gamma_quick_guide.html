Resource 07 · ChatGPT-to-Gamma Quick Guide

From ChatGPT Outline to Gamma Presentation

A practical guide for the 30-minute Gamma segment of the workshop. Build and review your content in ChatGPT first, then generate and refine the deck in Gamma so the design serves a clear, verified story.

All resources

Why combine ChatGPT and Gamma?

ChatGPT and Gamma solve different parts of the presentation problem. ChatGPT is strong at framing the objective, shaping a logical storyline, tightening language and checking for gaps or repetition. Gamma is strong at turning a clean outline into a designed set of cards quickly, with themes, layouts and visuals you can refine rather than build from a blank slide.

If you jump straight into Gamma with vague notes, the tool will still produce something that looks like a presentation — but it may look polished while carrying a weak story, duplicated points or unsupported claims. Doing the thinking in ChatGPT first means the deck you generate is already accurate, well-ordered and free of filler. Design then becomes a finishing step, not a rescue mission.

Use this sequence as your default workplace habit: decide the outcome, draft the outline with AI support, review every fact yourself, convert the outline into short Gamma-ready lines, generate the deck, then edit for clarity and consistency before you export or share.

The 13-step workflow

Work through these steps in order. Skipping early steps usually creates more rework later in Gamma.

  1. Define the presentation objective.

    Write one clear sentence on what the presentation must achieve by the end. Decide whether you need the audience to understand a situation, approve a recommendation, take a specific action, or simply stay informed. A vague objective such as “update the team” usually produces a vague deck; a sharp objective such as “brief team leads on the new handoff checklist and confirm the go-live date” gives ChatGPT and Gamma a usable target.

  2. Identify the audience.

    Note who will see the presentation, what they already know, and what they care about most. A leadership briefing needs different depth and language from a team walkthrough or a cross-functional update. Capture seniority, familiarity with the topic, and any decisions they are expected to make. Feed this audience description into your prompt so the tone, jargon level and level of detail stay appropriate.

  3. Decide the number of cards or slides.

    Match length to the time available and the decision you need. For a short internal update, six to eight cards is often enough; a denser briefing may need ten to twelve. Avoid asking for twenty slides when you have ten minutes to present. Fewer cards with one idea each are easier to deliver and easier for Gamma to design cleanly than a long deck packed with overlapping points.

  4. Ask ChatGPT to create the storyline.

    Use the reusable prompt in this resource. Fill in the number of slides, topic, audience and objective before you send it. Ask for a slide title, one key message, up to three supporting points and a suggested visual for each card. This structure keeps the outline presentation-ready instead of producing a long essay you later have to cut down by hand.

  5. Review facts and logical flow.

    Treat the ChatGPT outline as a draft, not a finished brief. Check every claim, date, name and figure against approved sources. Read the cards in order and ask whether a new colleague would understand the story without extra explanation. Remove duplication, fill gaps, and rewrite any point that sounds confident but is not supported by information you can verify.

  6. Convert the outline into Gamma-ready content.

    Shorten titles and keep each card to a key message plus two or three concise lines. Aim for bullets under twelve words so Gamma does not generate dense text blocks. Prefer concrete language over filler phrases. If a card still needs a paragraph to make sense, split it into two cards or move detail into speaker notes rather than crowding the slide.

  7. Generate the presentation in Gamma.

    Paste only the reviewed, Gamma-ready content. Do not paste confidential source documents, raw meeting notes or unverified statistics. Give Gamma a clear outline and let it build the first version of the deck. Generation should feel like formatting a finished brief, not inventing the narrative for the first time.

  8. Select a suitable theme.

    Choose a clean, professional theme that fits a corporate audience. Prefer simple backgrounds, readable contrast and restrained accent colours over busy patterns or decorative styles. A calm theme helps the content lead; a noisy theme competes with your message and can look less credible in internal or leadership settings.

  9. Edit layouts and cards.

    Adjust structure so each card carries one idea. Merge cards that repeat the same point and split cards that try to cover two decisions at once. Check that titles describe the point of the card, not a vague label such as “Overview” or “Details”. Layout edits are where you protect the storyline you already approved in ChatGPT.

  10. Replace unsuitable visuals.

    Swap any image that is decorative, off-topic, culturally odd or simply unclear. A relevant simple diagram, process arrow or icon set is usually better than a generic stock photo. If a visual does not help the audience understand the point in a few seconds, replace it or remove it. Never keep a visual only because Gamma generated it.

  11. Reduce excessive text.

    Trim each card to its key message and supporting points. Cut adjectives, repeated openings and any line that restates the title. If you need to say more while presenting, put that detail in your spoken remarks rather than on the card. Dense slides slow delivery and encourage the audience to read instead of listen.

  12. Check consistency.

    Confirm consistent tone, terminology, capitalisation, date formats and spacing across the deck. Use the same name for the same process or team every time. Align heading styles and bullet length so the deck feels intentional. Inconsistency is a common giveaway that content was generated quickly and not finished by a human editor.

  13. Export or share the final presentation.

    Do a last human review on screen or on print preview before anyone else sees it. Confirm the objective is still clear on the opening cards and that the closing card states the ask or next step. Only then export or share. AI can accelerate drafting and design; accountability for the final file stays with you.

Reusable presentation prompt

Copy the prompt below into ChatGPT. Replace the bracketed fields with your own details, then review the outline before you paste anything into Gamma.

Copy and adaptAct as a corporate presentation strategist. Create a [NUMBER]-slide presentation outline on [TOPIC]. Audience: [AUDIENCE] Objective: [OBJECTIVE] For every slide provide: - Slide title - One key message - Maximum three supporting points - Suggested visual Requirements: - Maintain a clear storyline - Avoid repetition - Keep each bullet below 12 words - Use professional, audience-appropriate language - Do not add unsupported information After creating the outline, review it for logical flow, duplication, clarity and suitability for Gamma.

Six-slide sample structure

Use this shape for a short internal presentation. The worked example below is a fictional process-improvement briefing for an internal operations team. It contains no real confidential data — replace every line with your own reviewed material before you present.

Slide 1 · Title and purpose

Key message: what this presentation is about and why it matters now.
  • Handoff Checklist Pilot — Mid-Year Update
  • Brief team leads on progress and next decision
Suggested visual: clean title card with team or process icon.

Slide 2 · Context or the problem

Key message: the situation the audience should understand first.
  • Handoffs between shifts still miss key checks
  • Rework rises when notes are incomplete
  • Leads asked for one shared checklist
Suggested visual: simple before/after problem diagram.

Slide 3 · Main idea one

Key message: the first thing you want them to take away.
  • Pilot ran for six weeks across two teams
  • Checklist covers five mandatory handoff points
  • Completion logged at each shift change
Suggested visual: short numbered checklist graphic.

Slide 4 · Main idea two

Key message: the second key takeaway.
  • Missed handoff items fell during the pilot
  • Teams report clearer ownership at changeover
  • One wording change still needed on Step 3
Suggested visual: simple comparison or trend callout.

Slide 5 · What it means / next steps

Key message: what the audience should do or remember.
  • Approve rollout to remaining day-shift teams
  • Finalise Step 3 wording by Friday
  • Leads coach new users for two weeks
Suggested visual: three-step next-action strip.

Slide 6 · Recap and questions

Key message: reinforce the single most important point.
  • Pilot improved handoff completeness; ready to scale
  • Decision needed: approve wider rollout this week
  • Questions and risks welcome before we proceed
Suggested visual: clean closing card with one clear ask.

Pre-Gamma review checklist

Complete these checks on the ChatGPT outline before you generate anything in Gamma.

  • Objective and audience are written clearly.
  • Slide count matches the time available.
  • Every fact, date and figure has been verified.
  • The storyline flows with no logical gaps.
  • No duplicated slides or repeated points.
  • Each card has one key message only.
  • Bullets are short (under 12 words).
  • No unsupported statistics or claims.
  • Content is approved and safe to paste into Gamma.

Post-Gamma review checklist

Complete these checks on the designed deck before you export or share.

  • Theme is clean, readable and professional.
  • Each card still carries one clear idea.
  • Titles describe the point, not vague labels.
  • Visuals are relevant, not merely decorative.
  • Text is trimmed; nothing feels crowded.
  • Tone and terminology are consistent throughout.
  • Opening purpose and closing ask are clear.
  • No confidential or unverified content remains.
  • A final human review is complete.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Generating slides before the content has been reviewed for accuracy. A designed deck makes weak or wrong content look finished. Errors become harder to notice and more damaging once they sit inside a polished layout that colleagues may trust at face value.
  • Overcrowding cards with long paragraphs instead of key points. Dense text forces the audience to read instead of listen, slows your delivery and often causes Gamma to choose cramped layouts. Short bullets keep attention on the spoken message.
  • Keeping visuals that look attractive but do not support the message. Decorative images create false confidence and can distract from the decision you need. Irrelevant visuals also waste the few seconds an audience spends scanning each card.
  • Letting the deck decide the story. If the template or auto-layout leads, you may accept a sequence that looks neat but does not answer the objective. The reviewed outline should lead; design should follow.
  • Skipping the final human read before sharing. Small issues in names, dates, ownership or next steps undermine credibility quickly in a workplace setting. A short final pass catches what generation and theme changes can introduce.
  • Including any unverified figure or claim the audience might rely on. People may act on what they see in a slide. Unsupported numbers or confident-sounding claims create operational and reputational risk even when the rest of the deck is sound.
Responsible use
Do not paste confidential, personal, clinical, proprietary or regulated information into public AI tools without approval. Use fictional, anonymised or explicitly approved content when practising this workflow. Verify every fact on every slide. AI output from ChatGPT or Gamma is a draft until a human reviews it, and final accountability remains with the person who shares the presentation.
